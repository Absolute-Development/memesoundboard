The Antikythera mechanism, dubbed as the first computer, is an angular device used to replicate the motions in the sky. With the device one is able to track the paths of planets, the moon, and the sun. The device acted like a clock, displaying celestial time, and had rotating hands for Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn. On the back were two dials, one for a calendar and the other for timing the solar and lunar eclipses. Scientists used imaging technology and x-ray scanning on 82 fragments, uncovering what was before unreadable text, to determine the purpose of the device.

Aryabhata was responsible for writing the Aryabhatiya, a book of astronomy and mathematics. Although he was wrong, he believed that the Earth spun on its own axis and the sun revolved around it. He believed the order of planets was The Moon, Mercury, Venus, the Sun, Mars, Jupiter, then Saturn. Mercury, Venus, and the Sun were thought to revolve around the Earth at the same speeds, while the other planets moved at their own pace. His calculation of a sidereal rotation was almost spot on and his sidereal year was only off by less than 4 minutes. Aryabhata used the sunrise to tell the start of the day as opposed to midnight like it is now. Aryabhata accurately found pi up to 4 decimal places. He started the use of zero as a place holder and for a number with no value. He found the area of a triangle.
